When my five-year-old self first learned about climate change, it did not seem like a threat. This changed once I learned more about its catastrophic effects. Over time, I started experiencing some of these firsthand. I saw the lush forests and meadows on my mountain hikes fall dry and barren. I even felt the effects of global warming as summer heat waves grew more frequent and 40°C days became the norm.

My concerns about climate change found a partner in my fascination with nature. I go hiking most weeks out of the year. While I enjoy the physical challenge of a good climb, my primary motivation is to experience natural beauty. Tectonic movements and uplift have turned the sea floor into mountain crests, creating a dramatic multiplicity of rocks and landforms. I love exploring these formations to no end.

My love for the Earth and my concerns about our treatment of her have coalesced in a strong interest in environmental and geological sciences. This interest deepened during the recent Geography Olympiad. During the finals, I trekked through various trails, local residences, and tourist spots in Xindian and examined how coal mining culture and fluvial erosion influenced local land use patterns. Afterwards, I came up with several evidence-based plans for flood prevention and devised sustainable methods to preserve cultural heritage sites through tourism. This experience cemented my desire to study climate change and environmental preservation. Purdue’s Environmental Geosciences major would completely fulfill that desire.
