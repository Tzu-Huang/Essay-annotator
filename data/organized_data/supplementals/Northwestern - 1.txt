
Northwestern is a place where people with diverse backgrounds from all over the world can study, live, and talk with one another. This range of experiences and viewpoints immeasurably enriches learning. How might your individual background contribute to this diversity of perspectives in Northwestern’s classrooms and around our campus?
	When I was born, our house was surrounded by farmland. As I grew up, our community was redeveloped into a sparkling technology district, and I enrolled in the newly built school on a scholarship. Unlike my friends’ parents, who all had advanced degrees, my parents never graduated from high school or learned English. Having access to the resources and community brought by “gentrification,” I became modernized.
Fascinated by the world springing up around me, I self-studied for AP economics and the International Economics Olympiad. Yet the more I learned, the more I realized how little I knew compared to my "uneducated" father, a small businessman who struggled for years before achieving stability manufacturing umbrellas. Whenever we discussed new theories I had learned from the textbook, he identified logical leaps and gaps in my understanding.
These experiences keep me grounded. I will never forget that great notions, from the redevelopment of an “underutilized” district to Schumpeter’s theory of creative destruction, are best understood by the people who live through them. While macroeconomic indicators and equations may give an accurate overview, true wisdom comes from everyday people, whether they are customers, entrepreneurs, or simply those whose village is going to be “modernized.”
