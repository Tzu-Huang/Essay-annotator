Aromatic vapor flooded my senses as I lifted the hefty iron lid of the rice cooker. I was greeted by the pleasant sight of steamed egg, its perfectly smooth surface glistening in the dull kitchen light like polished yellow jade. Grandpa had always been in charge of preparing meals at home- from porridge to rice cakes, his cooking always managed to light up our faces with joyful expressions of contentment. His steamed eggs were my favorite– despite its simple recipe, the tender and delicate texture was a spoonful of comfort that balanced the savory relish and rich, buttery taste of eggs. However, as Grandpa aged, the flavor of the steamed eggs became inconsistent by the day. On certain days, the eggs tasted as if someone had dumped in a gallon of the Dead Sea’s salt, while on others, they returned to their usual flavor. 

I watched his leathery hand tighten around the blackened bottle of soy sauce, tipping it to allow a mahogany stream into the pot of scrambled eggs. But just as quickly, Grandpa's hand began to tremble- large, sudden jerks that sent a tide of soy sauce tumbling and sloshing over the porcelain egg yolk surface. His brows furrowed in frustration, lips pressed into a thin line as the look of defeat crept into the crevices of his face. 

This was a harsh reminder of his worsening Parkinson’s. Each day, I watched helplessly as he lost more control. 

	Years later, as I stood in front of an operating table, draped in a white lab coat with latex gloves clinging uncomfortably to my skin, I thought back to those moments with Grandpa– the agonizing rigidity in his elbows and the uncontrollable tremors plaguing the pair of hands that had taken care of me for countless years. Armed with a cotton swab in one hand and forceps in another, I worked carefully to locate the bregma– a small, delicate cross on the skull that marked the spot where I would induce traumatic brain injury. Never had I imagined myself in a white coat, especially not on a random Monday afternoon with a Sprague Dawley rat mounted before me. Yet, as I searched for answers in the brain of this tiny creature, I realized that I was no longer a witness to neurodegeneration; I was now contributing to scientific research, driven by the same determination and empathy that had begun in my Grandpa’s kitchen. 

This was the day that I knew I wanted to be a neurosurgeon. 

I fell in love with the artistry of surgery, of tailoring the intricate biology of the brain with scalpels and sutures to potentially restore– or explore– its functions. During a graduate course lecture on the development of feral neural circuits, I encountered the subject of neural tube defects- a congenital condition that causes deformities of the central nervous system. These defects, often linked to nutritional deficiencies and inadequate maternal care, are especially prevalent in developing regions. As I absorbed the lecture, the images of children born with these disorders reminded me of the helplessness I felt watching my Grandpa’s struggle with Parkinson’s. The familiar ache sharpened my determination to make a difference. My goal became clear: to serve as a doctor without borders, bringing medical care to those who need it the most, where small interventions change entire lives. 

	As I reflect on my journey, my mind travels back to that kitchen, where the scent of steamed eggs filled the air and Grandpa’s trembling hands struggled against a body that was no longer under his control. His strength had inspired my own journey. Just as his cooking brought joy to our family, I now seek to bear the same comfort to others through medicine. What started as a love for a simple meal has grown into a lifelong passion for healing– a path of compassion, dedication, and the pursuit of knowledge that knows no bounds.
