At a young age, this was my introduction to medical imaging. Eyes glued to screen, I was mesmerized by how a culmination of plastics and circuit boards revealed the genesis of new life, all through a probe gliding smoothly along an expectant woman’s stomach. With my father often absent and my mother always busy, most of my after-school hours were spent at the gynecology clinic she worked at. The four walls of her office quickly became the cradle for my interest in medical imaging as I quietly observed her work, absorbing the process of monitoring the earliest stages of our lives.

My fascination with medical imaging continued as a guiding light throughout high school, serving as the fuel behind countless cold emails until I was accepted as a research assistant at a preclinical brain imaging lab. Under whirling MRI machines that unravel the secrets of brain autonomy, I learned to apply medical imaging to another context—investigating the neuroplasticity of adolescent rats. Over the summer, my eyes were opened to the potential of neuroscience in transforming patient outcomes. 

I continued to pursue my developing interest in neuroscience through a graduate-level course, where I learned about the prominence of congenital disorders in developing countries. As I prepared for a presentation on neural tube defects, I discovered the devastating effects of holoprosencephaly, a fetal developmental disorder often linked to maternal folate deficiency in developing countries. As I sifted through articles, the haunting images of affected children brought me to a heartbreaking realization that much of their suffering could have been prevented with adequate resources and healthcare. 

This was the day I knew that I wanted to be a neurosurgeon. 

Neuroscience is not just about understanding the brain, but bringing hope to those in need. My ultimate goal is to serve as a doctor without borders, and I know my next step as a neuroscience major will equip me with the tools to transform vision into reality.

On my 7th birthday, I discovered the first thing I truly hated: a spirograph set. 

I eagerly positioned the tip of my pencil in the gear, tracing around the jagged teeth, excited to replicate the intricate patterns advertised on the box. But as the lines danced across the sheet of paper, they seemed to follow their own chaotic will, refusing to obey the orders from my hands. When I lifted the tool, I was met with a jumbled mess of dismay. Frustrated, I traded the boxful of empty promises in favor of markers and paints—tools that bent to the will of my imagination, where every stroke was mine to command. 

However, I learned to appreciate the same chaos years later in front of the podium. The first thing I discovered about debate was that no matter how meticulously I arm myself with rebuttals, the opposing team will always find a way to tear down my carefully plotted line of defense. My first debate centered around the cliché topic of whether technology use should be permitted in classrooms. After my opponent presented a laundry list of potential harms, I was momentarily at a loss for words. Then, a thought crystallized in my head—I argued that the presence of risks does not justify an outright ban—much like it would be unreasonable to bad oranges simply because a child might choke on a seed. The analogy turned the tide of the round and showed me what real creativity entailed: creativity isn’t about mastering control but finding freedom within unpredictability and allowing the weeds of innovative thinking to sprout within the faults of preplanned structure.

This realization has molded not only my way of approaching debate but the process of tackling challenges in all aspects of my life. Whether it’s coming up with a new strategy to tackle a challenging sudoku puzzle or bridging incompatible groups together, I now see obstacles as a way to thrive. The motto of the spirograph now serves as the guiding light through my life: embrace the mess, work with the unexpected, and trust in the spontaneity that emerges. 

Prompt7. What have you done to make your school or your community a better place?

The first time I met Dasha, the static of our language barrier felt almost insurmountable. Our conversation began through Google Translate; my fingers scrambled clumsily across the keyboard, stringing together basic phrases and greetings into the tiny box that bridged together our worlds.

Dasha is a Ukrainian refugee, but more importantly, she is the first student I ever tutored.  
Initially, our differences seemed daunting. Yet, we quickly discovered a shared love for horror movies and cheesy creepypastas after reading an article on the mummification process from ancient Egypt. Watching short films together became a common routine in our sessions, and we frequently traded urban legends from our own cultures, like the tales of Poludnitsa, a Slavic spirit, or ghost stories rooted in Taiwanese traditions. 

As we spent more time together, Dasha started to open up to me. She vented about the uncertainty of her future and the wounds left by war. I did my best to support her, letting her know that she was understood, even from afar. It was in these moments of vulnerability that I realized the most meaningful support wasn’t found in perfecting English grammar, but in simply being there to listen.

Dasha has shown me that compassion is a language transcending beyond cultural or linguistic boundaries. Inspired by her resilience, I joined various international service events, raising funds for children impacted by similar crises. Through Christmas cookie sales and volunteering at a makeshift nail salon, we raised a total of 7,000 NTD—small actions that, to me, held immense meaning. What I didn’t anticipate was how these efforts rippled through my community. My friends, seeing my dedication, began to join. Together, we reached more people than I could have alone by dedicating spare hours during our weekends to volunteering as tutors. By leading through action, I felt the power of turning compassion into tangible action and the impacts of our collective empathy. 

Moving forward in my medical career, I will carry the same philosophy that Dasha has taught me, treating not just the illness but the person behind it, creating change that inspires others to act.
